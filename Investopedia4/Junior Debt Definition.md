## What Is Junior [[Debt]]?

Junior [[Debt|debt]] refers to bonds or other forms of [[debt]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/debt.asp) issued with a lower priority for repayment than other, more senior debt claims in the case of [[default]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/default2.asp). Because of this, junior debt tends to be riskier for investors, and thus carries higher interest rates than more senior debt from the same issuer.

Junior [[Debt|debt]] is synonymous with [[subordinated debt]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/subordinateddebt.asp), and it may refer more generally to any second tier of debt paid immediately following the repayment of senior debt. Junior debt has a somewhat smaller probability of being paid back in [[Default|default]] since all higher-ranking debt [[Will|will]] be given priority.

### Key Takeaways

-   Junior [[Debt|debt]] refers to bonds or other debts that have been issued with lower priority than senior debt.
-   Also known as subordinated [[Debt|debt]], junior debt [[Will|will]] only be repaid in the event of [[Default|default]] or bankruptcy after more senior debts have been first repaid in full.
-   Unlike senior [[Debt|debt]], junior debt is not typically backed by any type of [[Collateral|collateral]].
-   As a result of these attributes, junior [[Debt|debt]] tends to be riskier and carry higher interest rates than senior debt.

## Understanding Junior [[Debt]]

Generally, the corporate [[Debt|debt]] market is less regulated than the [[Equity Market|equity market]]. Thus, corporations have more flexibility obtaining capital through debt. A corporation may work with a bank to obtain a [[Loan|loan]]. They may also work with an underwriter who leads a loan syndicate with multiple investors investing in a loan deal. A corporation may also issue [[bonds]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bond.asp) with varying repayment terms.

"Junior [[Debt|debt]]" is a classification that is important for fixed income investors to understand when understanding the various bond issuances of a firm. [[Repayment priorities]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/absolutepriority.asp) for a business are a part of the firmâ€™s capital structuring, and these distinctions [[Will|will]] matter if an issuer experiences a [[credit event]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/credit-event.asp) such as a [[Default|default]]. Companies can issue a wide variety of securities to raise capital from investors, and the structuring of these products is typically done by an underwriter. The priority of repayment will generally follow the order of senior debtholders followed by junior debtholders, preferred shareholders, and finally common stockholders.

Different from [[Equity|equity]] capital, institutional [[Debt|debt]] is typically issued in the primary market involving direct interaction between corporations and investors. Following primary market issuance, loans and bonds can then be traded over secondary markets with trades facilitated through various trading groups. In the secondary market, senior debt continues to carry less [[Risk|risk]] than subordinated debt.

## [[Debt]] Repayment Terms

An important repayment term for all types of credit is their repayment seniority. Loans and bonds can be issued as senior [[Debt|debt]] or subordinated debt. Senior debt is repaid first if the borrower encounters a [[Default|default]] or liquidation. It is usually secured debt with [[collateral]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/[[Collateral|collateral]].asp); however, it can also be [[Unsecured|unsecured]] with specific provisions for repayment seniority. Subordinated debt follows senior debt and has its own repayment terms.

Generally, senior [[Debt|debt]] requires lower interest payments and bond coupons since it has a lower [[Risk|risk]]. With subordinated debt, investors are willing to take on the higher risk of lower seniority payments in [[Default|default]] by being compensated with higher rates of interest. Generally, junior debt and subordinated debt is [[Unsecured|unsecured]] debt that is not backed by [[Collateral|collateral]].

## Subordinated [[Debt]] in [[Tranches]]

In some situations, corporations may issue junior [[Debt|debt]] bonds. Junior debt can also be common in [[structured products]](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/optioninvestor/07/structured_products.asp) where investors have the option to invest in varying bond [[tranches]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/[[Tranches|tranches]].asp) as part of bond issuance. Repayment terms are often a key factor that can influence coupon rates on a bond. The junior debt repayment procedures in the case of [[Default|default]] [[Will|will]] be clearly delineated by the underwriter in the terms disclosing the investment details of a bond investment so that investors have a clear understanding of the priority the bonds are given in the case of default.

For instance, in many structured products, the [[z-tranche]](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/[[Z|z]]/ztranche.asp) is the slice of the [[Security|security]] that is repaid only after all other [[Tranches|tranches]] have received repayment in full.
